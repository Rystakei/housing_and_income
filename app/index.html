Housing and Affordability in the U.S.

How affordable is housing? The answer varies based on the area of the U.S. But first, what is the definition of affordable? One way to look at it is the ratio of the median household salary to the median price of a single famliy home in a given area. When graphed this way, there is a lot of variation in how large this ratio is, as seen below on the chart which plots the data of the 50 most populous U.S. cities.

At the extremes, there is Detroit, which has a ratio of 1.32 times the median income to purchase a house, and San Francisco, which has a ratio of 112.49 times the median income in the area. But how do the other cities do in terms of affordability?

What constitutes the ideal income to price gap when looking at houses depends on who you ask. Traditionally, experts have recommended that you don't exceed 4 times your income, and to aim to purchase a house that costs 2.5 to 3 times your income.

When you look at the graph, you can see that only 15 of the 50 most populous cities have a ratio that would allow a household with a median income to comfortably purchase a house that is around the median amount. Housing prices have greatly outpaced the growth of the median household's income, and as a result mortgage lenders instead calculate the front and back end debt load you have to allow greater leeway in determining how much housing you can "afford", resulting in people being deemed "qualified" to purchase homes that are 5, 6, or even 7 times their incomes.
